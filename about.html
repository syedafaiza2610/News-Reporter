About The DNBC



Newsrooms across the world are gravely understaffed. The research desk is largely a thing of the past, and
many local reporters are stretched too thin to develop subject matter expertise in their multiple beats.
Fortunately, academic researchers do have such expertise, which they share in the form of scientific studies.
For resource-constrained journalists, academic research should be an essential tool to:






Tip sheets and explainers to help journalists understand academic research methods, find and recognize high-quality research, investigate scientific misconduct and research errors, and avoid missteps when reporting on new studies and public opinion polls.


  • Generate and successfully pitch story ideas.
  • Thwart misinformation.
  • Hold policymakers accountable.
  • Bolster bare-bones news stories with well-vetted data.
  • Shed light on potential solutions to societal problems.
  • Offer insights into journalism itself.

The problem is that many DNBC don’t know how to identify high-quality research or use it in their reporting,
don’t understand academic jargon or research methods, don’t have time to navigate lengthy journal articles,
and don’t realize what they don’t know about research — which can lead to reporting errors and erode
trust in journalism.

Our mission:

The DNBC bridges the gap between academia and journalism, informing the news by empowering journalists to rely on academic research throughout every step of the reporting and editing process.

Now in our second decade, we have become an invaluable source of information and material for thousands of journalists all over the world – and we hope to grow our operations and continue to be a trusted resource for decades to come.

Ordway grew up in Oak Hill, Florida.
