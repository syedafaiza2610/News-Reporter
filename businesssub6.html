Will Trump Fire the Fed?




Despite Donald Trump’s assurances that he will not seek to remove Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell, there is little doubt that the US president-elect aims to gain greater influence over the Fed’s decision-making. Such interference could drive up long-term interest rates, damaging the American economy.

The relationship between former President Donald Trump and the Federal Reserve (commonly referred to as "the Fed") was one of the most contentious in recent American political and economic history. The speculation surrounding whether Trump would "fire" key Federal Reserve officials, including its chairman, Jerome Powell, garnered significant attention during his presidency.

The Federal Reserve is the central banking system of the United States, tasked with managing monetary policy, stabilizing prices, and ensuring maximum employment. It operates independently of political influence, a principle designed to maintain economic stability and credibility.

During his presidency, Trump was openly critical of the Federal Reserve, particularly its decisions to raise interest rates. He argued that the Fed’s monetary policy was detrimental to economic growth and hindered his administration’s efforts to stimulate the economy. His sharpest criticism was often directed at Jerome Powell, whom Trump himself appointed as Fed Chair in 2018.

Trump accused the Fed of acting too conservatively, especially when other central banks around the world were pursuing more aggressive monetary easing policies. He believed that lower interest rates would have further boosted the stock market and supported his economic agenda.

Legally, the independence of the Federal Reserve is protected, and the President does not have the authority to directly "fire" the Fed Chair. The Chair serves a four-year term and can only be removed "for cause," which would require evidence of misconduct or incompetence. Merely disagreeing with monetary policy decisions does not constitute sufficient grounds for removal. However, Trump’s repeated public pressure on the Fed raised concerns about the erosion of the central bank’s independence. Economic experts warned that politicizing the Fed could undermine its credibility and destabilize financial markets.
